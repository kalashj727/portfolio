# Quantitative Easing and the Wealth Gap: What the Transmission Mechanism Actually Does
*Finologix research note · by Kalash Jain*

When a central bank "prints money" through Quantitative Easing (QE), the popular story is that it floods the economy with cash and lifts everyone. The mechanism is subtler — and *who* it lifts is exactly the controversy. QE is the clearest modern example of a policy that can support the economy in aggregate while widening the gap between asset-owners and everyone else. Understanding the transmission channels is how you separate the politics from the economics.

## What QE actually is
In a normal downturn, a central bank cuts its policy interest rate. When that rate is already near zero, it has no more room. QE is the next lever: the central bank creates reserves and buys financial assets — mostly long-dated government bonds, sometimes corporate bonds or other securities — from banks and investors. The goals are to push **long-term interest rates down**, raise asset prices, and ease financial conditions when conventional rate cuts are exhausted.

## The channels — and where inequality creeps in

**1. The asset-price (portfolio rebalancing) channel.** By buying bonds, the central bank pushes their prices up and yields down. Investors holding the cash from those sales rotate into other assets — equities, corporate bonds, property — pushing *those* prices up too. This is the headline channel, and it is the one most directly tied to inequality: **the benefits accrue first and most to those who already own financial assets.** Stock and property ownership is heavily concentrated at the top of the wealth distribution, so a policy that lifts asset prices mechanically lifts the wealth of the already-wealthy more in absolute terms.

**2. The borrowing-cost channel.** Lower long rates reduce mortgage, corporate, and consumer borrowing costs. This is more broadly shared — homeowners refinance, firms invest, durable-goods purchases get cheaper. It can *narrow* gaps at the margin, especially for households with access to credit. (Note the catch: the poorest households often have little or no access to cheap credit, so they capture less of this benefit.)

**3. The labour-market (macro) channel.** If QE successfully supports demand, it lifts employment and wages. Because lower-income households are more exposed to unemployment, a stronger labour market can be **progressive** — it helps those at the bottom disproportionately. This is the central banks' main defence: "without QE, the recession would have been deeper, and downturns hurt the poor most."

## Why the net effect is contested
The honest answer is that QE's distributional impact is **a tug-of-war between channels (1) and (3)**:

- The **asset-price channel** tends to widen *wealth* inequality (stocks of assets).
- The **labour-market channel** tends to narrow *income* inequality (flows of wages and jobs).

So the conclusion depends on *which inequality you measure* (wealth vs income), *over what horizon*, and *what the counterfactual was* (a deeper recession). Several central banks have argued the income/employment benefits dominate, while many critics and independent studies emphasise the wealth-concentration effect. Both can be partly true at once — and that tension is precisely what makes this a good research question rather than a slogan.

## How to study it rigorously (and how I approached it)
For my coursework research I modelled the relationship between QE-era conditions and wealth-distribution measures using regression analysis. The methodological points that matter:

- **Separate wealth from income inequality** — they move differently and conflating them produces nonsense.
- **Define the counterfactual.** "Compared to no QE" is the only meaningful benchmark, and it is unobservable — which is why causal claims here are hard and estimates vary.
- **Beware endogeneity.** QE is deployed *because* the economy is weak, so naive correlations confuse cause and effect.
- **Mind the horizon.** Asset-price effects show up fast; labour-market effects show up slowly. A short window flatters the "QE worsens inequality" story; a longer window can soften it.

## The takeaway
QE is not a simple gift to the rich, nor a clean win for everyone — it is a policy whose first-order, fastest effect runs through asset prices (regressive for wealth) and whose slower, second-order effect runs through jobs and wages (progressive for income). Anyone who tells you the net sign is obvious is selling a narrative, not reading the evidence. The interesting work is in measuring *how much* of each, *for whom*, and *over what horizon*.
