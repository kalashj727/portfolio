# Reading a Bank's Health: A Practical Framework for Equity Research
*Finologix research note · by Kalash Jain*

A bank is not a normal company. It doesn't sell a product and book a margin — it *rents money*. It borrows from depositors and the market, lends at a higher rate, and the gap (minus losses) is the business. That single fact changes how you value it. A retailer is judged on revenue growth and operating margin; a bank is judged on **the quality of its loan book, the cheapness of its funding, and how much capital sits behind the risk.** Here is the framework I use to read one quickly.

## 1. Profitability — but the *right* profitability metrics

For most companies you look at operating margin and ROCE. For banks, the two numbers that matter most are:

- **Net Interest Margin (NIM)** = net interest income ÷ average interest-earning assets. This is the spread between what the bank earns on loans and pays on deposits. A higher, *stable* NIM signals pricing power and cheap funding.
- **Return on Assets (ROA)** and **Return on Equity (ROE).** Because banks run enormous balance sheets on thin spreads, ROA looks small (often around 1–2% for healthy banks) while ROE is amplified by leverage. Always read ROE *together* with leverage and capital — a sky-high ROE on thin capital is a warning, not a trophy.

Ask: is profitability coming from genuine spread and fee income, or from taking on riskier loans and under-providing for losses?

## 2. Asset quality — where banks actually die

Banks rarely fail because growth slowed. They fail because loans went bad faster than capital could absorb. Watch:

- **Gross and Net NPA ratios (GNPA / NNPA)** — the share of the loan book that has stopped performing. The *trend* matters more than the level.
- **Provision Coverage Ratio (PCR)** — how much of bad loans the bank has already set aside for. Low PCR means future losses are still lurking.
- **Slippage ratio** — the rate at which *good* loans are turning bad. This is the leading indicator; NPAs are the lagging one.
- **Restructured / stressed assets** — loans not yet classified as NPAs but clearly wobbling.

A bank growing its loan book fast while slippages creep up is borrowing tomorrow's losses to flatter today's growth.

## 3. Funding — the quiet competitive advantage

The cheapest, stickiest funding wins. The key gauge:

- **CASA ratio** (Current Account + Savings Account deposits ÷ total deposits). CASA money is low-cost and stable. A high CASA ratio is a structural moat — it lets a bank lend profitably without chasing risky high-yield loans. Banks with weak CASA lean on bulk/term deposits, which are pricier and flightier.

## 4. Capital and solvency — the shock absorber

- **Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR / CRAR)** measures capital against risk-weighted assets; regulators set a floor, and a comfortable buffer above it means the bank can absorb losses and keep lending through a downturn.
- A thin buffer means the next bad cycle forces dilutive capital raises — bad for existing shareholders.

## 5. Efficiency

- **Cost-to-Income ratio** — operating costs ÷ income. Lower is better; it shows how much of each rupee of income survives the running of the bank.

## 6. Valuation — why banks use P/B, not P/E

Equities are usually valued on P/E or DCF. Banks are most often valued on **Price-to-Book (P/B)**, because their assets and equity *are* the business and are marked close to realizable value. The discipline:

- A bank that consistently earns an **ROE above its cost of equity** deserves to trade *above* book value; one that earns below it should trade at a discount. P/B and ROE must be read as a pair.
- Cross-check with P/E and a dividend/earnings view, and always relative to peers and the bank's own history.

## The one-paragraph checklist
Is NIM healthy and stable? Are slippages and NPAs trending **down**, with adequate provisioning? Is funding cheap and sticky (strong CASA)? Is there a real capital buffer? Is the cost-to-income ratio competitive? And finally — does the P/B you're paying make sense given the ROE the bank actually earns versus its cost of equity? If the answers line up, you're likely looking at quality. If growth is fast but asset quality and capital are quietly deteriorating, be skeptical no matter how good the headline ROE looks.
